Grad School Jargon

When I was applying to different grad schools, there was a lot of jargon on the departmental admission pages. Words like “Comps,” “Candidacy,” “Assistantship,” etc. I had no idea what these things were! While I did eventually learn what (most) of these terms meant throughout grad school, it might have made my life a bit easier if I knew what these terms meant ahead of time. Therefore, I've listed below are some of the more common grad school jargon that I feel most students come across (especially during admission season).

Common Terminology

Advancement to Candidacy
At the beginning of a graduate program, a student is not yet considered a candidate for a graduate degree. In order to become a candidate, a student must have completed his/her academic and program requirements, as well as any needed examinations. The milestone of advancing to candidacy signifies that the student is ready to move forward and begin working on their dissertation and a “PhD/Grad Student” becomes a “PhD Candidate”
Assistantship
Most graduate programs offer assistantships and they are typically categorized as teaching assistantships (TA), research assistantships (RA) or graduate assistantships (GA). Essentially, the school provides students paid tuition, a monthly stipend and health insurance in exchange for completing certain types of work, such as teaching undergraduates.
Committee
Faculty members, in addition to your Research Advisor, that help to guide coursework and research. These members will also serve as your final examiners during your defense.
Defense
Typically an oral presentation where you give a summary of your thesis and the research results you obtained. An examining committee then asks you questions which you must respond to in order to defend your work.
